FAQs

We have provided some answers to some of your frequently asked questions.


For more information you can send us a message
or contact us.

1. Who can take the test?

Anyone, especially those who were exposed to people with COVID-19.

2. How does this rapid test work?

If the patient has contracted COVID-19, the biomarkers in their blood will bind to the antibodies on the test strip, leaving a visible test line.

If the patient does not have COVID-19, no biomarkers should be present in their blood and no test line will be visible.

3. What are IgG and IgM?

These immunoglobulins are antibodies and are part of our immune system.

When we get an infection, such as COVID-19, immunoglobulins are produced, which attach to the virus and activate the rest of the immune system to attack and clear the virus.

IgM is the first immunoglobin to be produced and is a general antibody that can bind to many different types of pathogens. The presence of IgM is an indicator of early infection. IgG is a more specialized antibody but specifically binds to the SARS-CoV-2 virus. The presence of IgG is and indicator of later-stage infection. (Usually 7 days or more after infection)

4. Is the test specific for COVID-19?

The IgG that the test detects is specific to COVID-19, so the positive result would indicate COVID-19 infection. Although the IgM is a more generalized antibody, its presence, combined with IgG and/or the common symptoms of COVID-19, would also indicate infection with the novel SARS-CoV-2 virus. The test can be used for primary and secondary diagnosis of COVID-19.

5. Can the test detect asymptomatic patients?

Yes, because they will have an immune response even without the symptoms.

6. What samples are used for the test?

Whole blood, plasma, or serum.

7. Can babies and young children be tested?

Yes, there will be no problem with that.

8. Can pregnant and breastfeeding women be tested?

Yes, there will be no harm for both mother and the baby.
